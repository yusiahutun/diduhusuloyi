Blackbeard the pirate was actually an aristocratic family man who gave up wealth

 Posted on September 26, 2024  |  12 minutes  |  2397 words  |  Aldo Pusey

He is one of the most notorious pirates to have ever lived, with a fearsome reputation for savagery and ruthlessness that left coastal settlements living in terror of his ships.

But a new study of historical records has suggested Blackbeard's ferocious notoriety may be misplaced and he was in fact a caring family man and respected war veteran.

The claims dispute many of the depictions of the notorious pirate as a vicious killer who marooned men on desert islands and reportedly prostituted his own wife in his pursuit of wealth.

Blackbeard, depicted in the painting above, had a fearsome reputation as a ruthless killer, but historical documents unearthed in Jamaica have shed new light on his background. They suggest he was a member of a aristocratic family and a caring family man who deeded his inherited wealth to his half brother and sister

Instead, they present a picture of Blackbeard as a member of a respected aristocratic family from Jamaica who gifted much of his own inheritance to his younger half-brother and half-sister.

Official records from Jamaica even suggest the man believed to be the pirate Blackbeard – Edward Thache or Teach – had a daughter who married a doctor on the islands.

Historian Baylus Brooks, who conducted the research with the North Carolina Office of Archives and History, also questions the validity of many of the exploits credited to the legendary pirate.

He argues many of the deeds attributed to Blackbeard, who became known by some as the King of Pirates, were misconceptions, or worse, made up entirely.

WHO WAS BLACKBEARD? 

Many of the pirates in the Caribbean began their careers as privateers in the employment of the British crown to plunder Spanish ships during Queen Anne's war against the country.

However, when the Hanovian king George was placed upon the throne on Queen Anne's death, the authorities sought to stamp out the privateers, effectively outlawing them. 

It is thought Edward Thache - believed to be Blackbeard - was one such privateer who had been working the wrecks of Spanish galleons on the coast of Florida when his commission was withdrawn by the crown. 

He was eventually given command of his own ship by fellow privateer Captain Benjamin Hornigold. 

Much of the legend surrounding Blackbeard stems from accounts written in a book called A General History of the Robberies and Murders of the Most Notorious Pyrates. 

It was published in 1724, some six years after Blackbeard's death in Ocracoke in 1718, by Captain Charles Johnson. 

Captain Johnson is said to be a pseudonym for a Jacobite called Nathaniel Mist.

Mist's book presents the notorious pirate as a vicious killer who marooned men on desert islands and reportedly prostituted his own wife in his pursuit of wealth. 

He blames a book that focused on Blackbeard for many of the inaccuracies.

'The enormously successful pirate Edward Thache was not the notorious and villainous "Pirate King" Blackbeard - or Satan "from hell" as Mist wrote him to be,' explained Mr Brooke.

'He was an educated aristocrat, likely grandson of an Anglican minister trained at Oxford.

'His family in Spanish Town, Jamaica, owned an estate with slaves; they probably rode in their personal carriage to church and conversed with assemblymen and their families in the palace square.

'And he was a veteran, having served his nation during Queen Anne's War aboard a massive sixty-gun frigate of the Royal Navy.'

However, Mr Brooks believes many of the details of Blackbeard's life may have been made-up by Mist as he lacked access to official records, and indeed he may have embellished many details.

In particular, he claims Mist was in fact often in trouble with the authorities himself and in debt, and may have been used by the British crown to help blacken the reputations of privateers such as Edward Thache.

DISPUTING BLACKBEARD CLAIMS

In Blackbeard Reconsidered, historian Baylus Brooks claims to have uncovered several official records that tell a different story about the life of Edward Thache.

Church of England documents from the parish of St Catherine's in Jamaica suggest the Thache family lived there from at least 1699.

A burial record shows an Elizabeth 'Theach' - Blackbeard's mother - died there in January 1699 and there was then a marriage for an 'Edward Theach' to Lucretia Ethell (Axtrell) in July 1699.

The records describe Edward Thache Jnr as serving aboard the HMS Windsor. 

They also show he deeded his inheritance from his father to his stepmother Lucretia.

The records state that he did this for the 'love and (a)ffection I have for and bear towards my brother and sister Thomas Theache and Rachel Teache'.

These are thought to have been his step brother and sister – the children his father had with Lucretia – while Edward was the result of his father's marriage to Elizabeth.

Mr Brooks also claims to have found evidence that suggests Blackbeard had a daughter with his wife before she died in around 1705, who he named Elizabeth after his own mother.

This girl is said to have been raised by Lucretia alongside her own young children.

Thache then went on to capture a French slave ship, La Concorde de Nantes, which he renamed the Queen Anne's revenge, and eventually had several ships under his command.

The English pirate had a fearsome appearance, tying his thick black beard into pigtails, stringing weapons from slings around his shoulders and sticking lighted matches under his hat when he went into battle.

He also had a reputation for marooning members of his crew and enforcing strict discipline on board his ships. 

He is even said to have deliberately run his flagship, the Queen Anne's Revenge aground in 1718 in Beufort Inlet, North Carolina.

SEVETEEN YEARS OF DISCOVERY

Experts have found an array of fascinating artifacts from Blackbeard's Queen Anne's Revenge wreckage since 1996.

Archaeologists have recovered onion bottles, two bells of Spanish or Portuguese origin a coin weight with Queen Anne's likeness stamped on it, buckles, glass beads, buttons, cuff links, pieces of wine glasses, a syringe, gold flakes, among others.

The same team are said to have also found 11 cannons from the pirate ship, including one in 2005 and another in 2007.

Another rare find was a partly gilded hilt thought to have held the sword of Blackbeard himself.

They describe Edward Thache Jnr as serving aboard the HMS Windsor.

DID BLACKBEARD CARE FOR HIS SICK AND INJURED CREW?

Treasures found among the wreckage of Blackbeard's ship, Queen Anne's Revenge, suggest the pirate may have had a caring side.

Marine archaeologists found evidence of medical equipment used to heal the sick and wounded on board the pirate captain's vessel.

The artefacts are the latest clues of what life would have been like on the ship that became feared along the east coast of America and the West Indies.

The wreckage was discovered in 1996 on a sandbar in Beaufort Inlet, North Carolina, where Blackbeard ran it aground in 1718.

Since then archaeologists working on the Queen Anne's Revenge project have recovered cannons, glass beads, coins, pottery and gold dust from the remains of the vessel that have provided a glimpse of the pirates' lifestyle.

But among the wreckage they have also found a number of items that would have been used for medical care, including a syringe, a blood porringer, an apothecary's weight set, along with a mortar and pestle.

Archaeologists who have been studying the artefacts said they suggest Blackbeard made great efforts to keep his crew healthy.

It is a image that contrasts with the pirate captain's ruthless and ferocious reputation.

'As a crewman in the Royal Navy, he probably did not need the plantation, but it would help his family to survive.

'He fished the Spanish wrecks on the Florida coast like many Jamaicans and followed the growing crowd of exiled ex-privateers to New Providence, where they became branded as "pirates, the "enemies of all mankind."'

Indeed, he argues that many of the stories that grew up around Blackbeard were probably the product of the Crown's attempts to crush piracy in the Caribbean at the time and sought to remove the support former privateers such as Thache had enjoyed among the local inhabitants.

Mr Brooks added: The Board of Trade likely believed the wealthy and ex-Royal Navy man Thache and his fellow privateers threatened their efforts to eradicate piracy in America.

'Its members would have sought to prevent Thache from gaining even more popularity and support from his well-born community as a pirate.

'They used every available measure to diminish his reputation. 

'Nathaniel Mist, suffering from repeated arrests and fines, may have caved to royal pressure and described 'Blackbeard' as the more 'villainous' character in order to accomplish this purpose.

'As long as few knew of his family and his service, he could be portrayed as wicked and dangerous.'
