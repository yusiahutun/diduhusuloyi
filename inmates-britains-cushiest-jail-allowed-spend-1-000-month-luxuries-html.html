Inmates at 'Britain's cushiest jail' are allowed to spend up to 1,000 a MONTH on luxuries

 Posted on July 24, 2024  |  4 minutes  |  686 words  |  Reinaldo Massengill

Inmates of Britain’s ‘cushiest’ jail can spend up to £250 a week from their cells, it has been revealed, despite fears it is fuelling violence and extortion among prisoners.

Electronic appliances, CDs, DVDs, clothes, toiletries, phone credits and groceries can be bought from a ‘spends account’ – made up of cash that inmates have earned in prison, transferred from personal accounts or had sent in by family and friends.

Critics last night said the revelation about HMP Berwyn provided fresh evidence that jails were becoming ‘holiday camps’.

Conservative MP Philip Hollobone said: ‘People will not believe prisoners are allowed to spend such a lot of money each week. It seems to be more Supermarket Sweep than a jail.

HMP Berwyn in Wrexham, Wales has been labelled 'Britain's cushiest prison' by some critics

‘The public will be horrified that prisons are being turned into glorified holiday camps. No wonder it has got the reputation as Britain’s cushiest prison.’

The scandal emerged in a report, published today, by the Inspectorate of Prisons into HMP Berwyn, a £212million super-prison on the outskirts of Wrexham, North Wales.

The jail, which holds about 1,300 men, is the largest in the country. It hit the headlines when it opened in 2017 after it emerged that prisoners are given telephones so they can say goodnight to their children and would be held in rooms, not cells, and that warders have to knock before entering. A wall in the jail has also been decorated with a copy of a mural by the artist Banksy which depicts a rioter throwing a bunch of flowers.

The inspectors’ report said inmates have laptops in their cells to arrange visits, order meals, buy food, drinks and toiletries and work on their studies – although they do not have access to the internet.

Inmates who have enough money in their spends accounts and have earned the privilege can purchase goods from catalogues or the prison shop.

Up to 1,000 items are on the list of products approved by HM Prison And Probation Service to make sure they do not pose a security or safety risk.

Under prison service rules, spending limits are linked to a prisoner’s behaviour, with those entering jail eligible to spend a maximum of £100, falling to £40 for the basic level and rising to £250 for well-behaved inmates.

But the report has warned the large sums available have created a risk of intimidation, bullying and extortion from other prisoners – dubbed ‘taxing’.

The watchdog added: ‘Prisoners could spend up to £250 of their money each week. This presented a real risk of extortion and bullying which, at the time of inspection, the prison had no processes to manage.’

Labour MP Ian Lucas, whose constituency covers HMP Berwyn, criticised the £250 a week spending limit.

He said: ‘I think most people would be very surprised that level of spending is even allowed. It is concerning. This could be fuelling violence in the prison.’

The measures were part of the Ministry of Justice’s vision to bolster rehabilitation and education levels among prisoners. However, drugs were ‘readily available’ at HMP Berwyn, said the report, with almost one in four inmates developing an addiction behind bars.

Russ Trent, the governor when it first opened who has since been replaced, said at the time he wanted to run it so inmates felt a sense of belonging.

Staff were also told not to refer to inmates as ‘prisoners’ or ‘offenders’.

Amy Rees, of the Prison And Probation Service, said: ‘The new governor in place since the inspection is already building on that progress, including through closer working with the police and better searching for illicit drugs, and introducing a new model that challenges poor behaviour and reduces violence.’
