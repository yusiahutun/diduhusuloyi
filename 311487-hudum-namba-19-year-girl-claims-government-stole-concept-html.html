Huduma Namba: 19-year-old girl claims government stole concept

 Posted on September 6, 2024  |  4 minutes  |  709 words  |  Aldo Pusey

- Mercy Kimani said she and her friend came up with a similar idea at Naivasha Girls High School with the help of their teacher

- They developed Mwananchi Card which is swiped through a magnetic card reader connected to a computer where information on card's owner is displayed

- The innovators presented the concept during Young Scientists Kenya exhibition in 2018 with the hope of getting support from government

- About eight months later, the government rolled out Huduma Namba, a technology that bore a similarity to the Mwananchi Card

A 19-year-old girl has accused the government of stealing concept behind the Huduma Namba technology which she claimed was similar to one she showcased during Young Scientists Kenya (YSK) exhibition in July 2018.

Mercy Kimani claimed she and her friend Lorraine Ouma were the first to come up with the social security card idea in Kenya and could not help but notice the government's Huduma Namba project was very similar to theirs.

The young innovator who spoke to Citizen TV on Saturday, July 27, revealed after taking a closer look at the Huduma Namba captured on the National Integrated Identity Management System (NIIMS), she discovered concept had components similar to their innovation dubbed Mwananchi Card.

"The moment I saw Huduma Namba on TV, at first it didn't capture my attention, but when I went to the NIIMS site and looked through their PDF, apparently Huduma Namba had so many similarities which I wouldn't consider coincidental," Mercy said.

The tech prodigy disclosed it took them months of hard work to come up with the concept with the help of their teachers at Naivasha Girls High School in Nakuru county.

"I had researched on the social security card and thought that it would be such a nice idea since in our country we did not have such," she said.

The social security number (Huduma Namba) technology is already being used in most developed country.

Naivasha Girls High School, according to Mercy, funded their project to the tune of about KSh 20,000.

"They (also) gave us time and allowed us to use the facilities, that is the computer hub. They also provided us with the materials we needed, that is the magnetic ink card reader and the cards themselves that we were to use," Mercy said.

The teenagers then developed the Mwananchi Card which is swiped through a magnetic card reader connected to a computer to display information on card's owner.

She and her project partner Lorraine were among finalists who were picked to present science projects during the YSK 2018 exhibitions at Kenyatta International Convention Centre (KICC).

Their wish was to meet President Uhuru Kenyatta so they could present to him their concept. Unfortunately, he did not come to their stand.

However, ICT Cabinet secretary Joseph Mucheru showed up and even recognised the young girls' project in his speech.

"The moment we saw the CS for ICT we were so excited," Mercy said.

After they explained the concept to him, the girls exchanged contacts with the CS and they were hopeful he would get in touch again.That, Mercy said, never happened. Efforts to reach Mucheru were also futile.

On his part, Mucheru advised schools should not only support but also manage students' expectations when coming up with ideas.

"The youths themselves have a responsibility to research and see 'is this thing of mine that I am thinking about new or has it existed elsewhere' so that they do not get disappointed," the CS told Citizen TV.

It was not immediately clear whether or not Mercy and Lorraine's concept had been patented.
