What is rosacea, and the best ways to treat it

 Posted on March 18, 2024  |  12 minutes  |  2348 words  |  Jenniffer Sheldon

Rosacea is thought of as a condition that involves redness of the face. And while it is, the symptoms are more complex than a bit of blushing. There are several different types of rosacea, all of which manifest on the skin in unique ways, and you could have one or a combination of them.

It’s a relatively common condition, with one in 10 estimated to have it in the UK – a figure that's thought to be increasing. "Like a number of skin conditions, rosacea cannot be cured, but it can certainly be managed and minimised," says consultant dermatologist Alexis Granite. "It’s important if you are suffering with rosacea to speak to a skincare professional because they can help get you on the right treatment path with individual prescriptions and skincare. Management of rosacea takes routine and perseverance, but improvement is absolutely possible," she adds.

When I was diagnosed with rosacea six years ago, I changed my entire skincare routine on the advice of the experts. I replaced my trend-led regime with minimal products, and to my pleasant surprise my redness started to ease. If you too have – or suspect – a rosacea diagnosis, here's everything you need to know about the complex skin condition…

What is rosacea?

"Rosacea is a skin condition that often presents as facial redness," confirms consultant dermatologist Dr Anjali Mahto, founder of Self London. "It begins with episodes of intermittent flushing – where the skin turns red for a short period – but over time it can become fixed.” In deeper complexions, this inflammation appears browner in tone.

What are the different types of rosacea?

The most common symptoms of rosacea are redness, flushing and breakouts, but the condition may manifest differently in each individual. Here, Granite breaks down the four main types of rosacea:

Erythematotelangiectatic rosacea or ‘subtype one’

This form of rosacea is mainly characterised by redness, blushing and broken capillaries.

Papulopustular rosacea, or ‘subtype two’

Here, red papules and pustules will appear on the skin, often over a background of erythema (or redness). This can often be confused with acne.

Rhinophymatous rosacea, or ‘subtype three’

This is a rarer, more severe form of rosacea that causes the skin to thicken, giving it a more bulbous appearance, particularly on the nose.

Ocular rosacea, or ‘subtype four’

This rosacea subtype causes redness and irritation within the eyes, as well as swollen eyelids.

What causes rosacea?

The cause of rosacea isn’t entirely known. Dr. Justine Hextall, consultant dermatologist at the Tarrant St Clinic, believes that a mixture of factors can contribute to the onset of rosacea. Things like genetics, environmental factors, gut health and stress can all play a part, which is why she offers a holistic action plan for her patients. Her treatments include everything from suggesting a gentle skincare routine and medical prescriptions to gut health screening and referral to a counsellor.

What are the most common rosacea triggers?

Anything that makes skin flush is a potential trigger of rosacea, and each patient will notice different factors. According to Dr. Granite, common triggers can include “alcohol, spicy foods and hot drinks, as well as stress, UV exposure, exercising, and extreme weather such as cold and wind”.

If you’re struggling to identify your triggers, my suggestion would be to keep a lifestyle diary. Make a note of how your skin has behaved along with documenting the things from Dr. Mahto’s list: How was the weather? What did you eat? Were you feeling more stressed than usual that day? Over time, you’ll start to identify patterns and can use this knowledge to your advantage. Let’s say, for example, one of your triggers is spicy food. You might then choose not pick it from a menu the night before a special occasion.

Is it acne or rosacea?

Papulopustular rosacea is often conflated with acne, as both manifest as spots on the skin. However, the two are not the same and should be treated very differently. “Rosacea tends to have a background of redness on the skin, particularly in the central area of the face, cheeks and nose. Acne tends to be associated with comedones (whiteheads and blackheads), which are not a characteristic feature of rosacea,” explains Dr. Granite. Such redness will often present in a butterfly shape across the centre of the face.

How can I treat rosacea at home?

After my diagnosis and armed with knowledge, I found that my skin looked better from using fewer products. A smaller, more affordable routine led to much less redness in my skin. Here are five things that I did that made the biggest difference.

1. Simplify your skincare routine

A basic routine should comprise of these steps: a hydrating cream cleanser, a hydrating moisturiser, a high factor, broad spectrum SPF for use in the morning, plus ideally a treatment step to reduce inflammation.

"A dermatologist will be able to prescribe anti-inflammatory creams that will calm redness, but in terms of products available on the market, look for washes, cleansers and moisturisers designed specifically for sensitive skin” says Dr. Mahto.

She also recommends avoiding products that contain fragrance – natural or otherwise, meaning you should keep an eye out for essential oils – as well as harsh exfoliators, toners and astringents. "Alcohol, retinoids, witch hazel, menthol and eucalyptus oil can also aggravate rosacea, as can acne treatments, so always test a patch on your skin before applying products to your face", she adds.

2. Become religious about applying SPF

As with all skin, wearing a broad-spectrum SPF every day is crucial. Rosacea sufferers have fragile capillaries which can be easily damaged by UV.

"It's extremely important to use sunscreen daily to help manage your rosacea, as UV radiation is one of the common triggers, meaning sunlight can cause flare-ups", says Dr. Mahto.

"Ideally an SPF of 50 that provides broad-spectrum UVA and UVB protection should be worn," she continues. "Special attention should be paid to the type of sunscreen you use, too. Mineral sunscreens containing physical blockers such as zinc and titanium dioxide are less likely to exacerbate rosacea-prone skin than chemical blockers such as avobenzones and benzophenones."

3. Seek professional guidance on the best treatment step for you

While rosacea cannot be cured, there are several treatment options to help manage the condition and its symptoms even further. In order to determine the right treatment step, first see a medical professional who will be able to diagnose the subtype of rosacea you are dealing with and recommend a treatment accordingly.

Doing this is easier than ever. Not long ago, your options were simply to see your GP or a dermatologist. While both are brilliant choices, there are now more ways to seek professional help.

To deal with spots associated with rosacea, your doctor or practitioner may prescribe a topical cream such as metronidazole, azelaic acid or ivermectin – typically applied once or twice a day.

Keep in mind that it may take several weeks before you notice any significant improvement in your symptoms, and that side effects vary per treatments and person.

4. Choose the right make-up

If your redness is bothering you, it’s perfectly safe to use make-up to cover it.

"While individuals with rosacea can wear make-up, look for multi-tasking products to reduce the amount of different ingredients you're applying to your skin, helping to avoid irritation," suggests Dr. Mahto.

In my 12-plus years of make-up artistry, I’ve found that a soft touch sounds so simple but can make an enormous difference to the look of your make-up when you have redness. Artist to the stars Lisa Eldridge agrees.

“I’m really careful with the skin. If I’m doing a facial massage or anything like that, I’m ever so gentle. Then I’m likely to do a light layer of colour all over, then I’ll mix my concealers and tend to use something a bit more olive (not that you’ll notice it). I often do most of the redness correction as the last layer.”

As for concealer, using a tiny brush can be great to help build extra coverage just where you need it and without overwhelming the entire face. 5. Moisturise your skin pre-emptively

Redness-prone skin can be hugely impacted by environmental factors. A cold walk or a hot shower, for example, can make my skin flush in an instant. To counteract this, in addition to my daily moisturising routine, I might add a bit more moisture before I enter extreme environments. It was a tip given to me by Dr. Hextall many years ago and one that has helped me enormously ever since.

Alternatively, if you’re wearing make-up for the day and want to do this but don’t want to remove your base, you could simply add moisture with a facial mist instead. The La Roche Posay Toleriane Ultra 8 Soothing Spray is like a moisturiser in a fine spritz.

What options are there to treat rosacea professionally?

Once you’ve explored these steps you should start to see a big reduction in your redness. If, however, you still want further support, here are some more options that your practitioner may recommend – and what to expect from them.

1. Oral antibiotics

If your symptoms are more severe, an oral, antibiotic medication may be recommended to help reduce inflammation. Antibiotics often used to treat rosacea include tetracycline, oxytetracycline, doxycycline and erythromycin. These medications are usually taken for four-to-six weeks, but longer courses may be necessary if the spots are persistent. For example, a low-dose doxycycline capsule is available for long-term use. I haven’t personally used these.

Common side-effects of these medications include: vomiting, diarrhoea, bloating and indigestion, abdominal pain and loss of appetite. These medications can also make your skin sensitive to artificial light and UV rays – so a good facial SPF is a must. As with the topical treatments, these medications usually need to be taken once or twice a day and you may not notice a significant improvement in your symptoms for several weeks.

2. Laser therapy

The flushing, redness and visible blood vessels associated with rosacea (particularly with the erythematotelangiectatic subtype) can be reduced with vascular lasers or IPL (intense pulsed light) treatment.

Laser and IPL machines produce narrow beams of light that are aimed at the visible blood vessels in the skin. The heat from the lasers damages the dilated veins and causes them to shrink so they're no longer visible, with minimal scarring or damage to the surrounding area.

A course of treatment is often more effective than a single session, so the overall cost can be significant. Laser treatment can cause some pain, but most people don't need an anaesthetic. This is something I am looking into for 2024, as a stressful 2023 is showing all over my face in the form of spidery thread veins.

Finally, remember that you can reduce your redness

All in all, I want to assure you from personal experience that you can drastically reduce your redness. Develop gentler habits and maintain your routine, and your skin will feel infinitely calmer.

Rosacea can be unpredictable, and sometimes you can do everything right and you’ll still have a flare-up. But by keeping things consistent, you can unlock your best skin yet.
